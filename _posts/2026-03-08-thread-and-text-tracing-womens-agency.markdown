On August 26, 1920, the 19th Amendment was ratified. Women could vote. This project asks: what changed in language?

Using computational text analysis, I placed two corpora in direct conversation: thirteen literary novels from 1865 to 1925, and legal texts from the same period (Supreme Court opinions, Congressional debate transcripts, and suffrage briefs). Together they form a record of how fiction and law wrote women during the same decades.

## The Method

The literary corpus includes works by Edith Wharton, Willa Cather, Kate Chopin, Ellen Glasgow, Booth Tarkington, and Sinclair Lewis, representing female- and male-authored texts across three windows: pre-Amendment, the year of passage, and five years after. Three tools are applied to both corpora:

- **Figurative language extraction**: similes and metaphors mapped to conceptual domains (nature, property, movement, constraint)
- **Emotional arc analysis**: NRC Emotion Lexicon applied chapter by chapter
- **Grammatical agency tracking**: how often the female protagonist is the subject of her own sentences versus the object of others' actions

## Early Findings

Mapped as a chord diagram, the acting verbs of each protagonist reveal what crosses period lines: *sat, stood, went, walked, rose* — a shared vocabulary of containment and threshold.

Agency ratio confirms the pattern: the year 1920 averages 34%, below the pre-1920 average of 43%, with Wharton's Ellen Olenska scoring the lowest in the corpus at 21%, written two months after the vote passed.

## The Physical Work

The chord diagrams generated by the analysis will be translated into fiber art and physical installation. Each chord becomes thread, weighted by frequency, colored by period and author gender, woven into a structure you can walk around and touch. A chord diagram on a screen shows you a relationship between a novel and a legal brief. A woven fiber installation asks you to stand inside it.

## Code

The analysis pipeline is written in Python using NLTK for tokenization and POS tagging, with D3.js for visualization. Code snippets are available on [GitHub Gist](https://gist.github.com/PowChow/8cac216f1bb3684d74f0bed5095f2b2d).
