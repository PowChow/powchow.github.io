Thread and Text:
Tracing Women's Agency
Across the 19th Amendment

A computational study of figurative language across literary fiction and legal texts in the five years before and after the 19th Amendment — asking whether law and literature told the same story about women, or different ones.

1915 – 1925 U.S. Literary & Legal Corpus NLP · D3 Visualization · Digital Humanities
The Question

Did the language change when the law did?

In the five years before and after the 19th Amendment, did fiction and law share a metaphorical vocabulary for women — or did literature imagine something that law had not yet found words for?

Law and literature have always shared a language problem. Both are made entirely of words. Both make arguments through metaphor. Both construct the world as much as they describe it. And yet we rarely read them together — not computationally, not at a scale that can surface the patterns invisible to any single reader moving through a single text.

The 19th Amendment creates a clean analytical break: a moment when a woman's legal personhood formally expanded. This project asks whether the language — the metaphors, the emotional textures, the conceptual associations — shifted with it, preceded it, or lagged behind.

01
Metaphor domains: What conceptual domains does fiction use to figure women — nature, property, movement, interiority? What does law use? Do they share a vocabulary or operate in separate imaginative universes?
02
Emotional texture: Does pre-Amendment legal writing carry different emotional signatures than post-Amendment writing? Does fiction anticipate the shift, or follow it?
03
The before/after delta: Did the language actually change after August 1920 — or did the words stay the same while the world changed around them?

The Corpus

Fiction and law, reading each other across the same decade

The target corpus pairs literary texts written by and about women with legal texts that defined what women were — produced in the same five-year windows on either side of August 26, 1920.

Literary Texts · 1915–1925

  • Summer — Edith Wharton (1917)
  • My Ántonia — Willa Cather (1918)
  • The Age of Innocence — Edith Wharton (1920)
  • Main Street — Sinclair Lewis (1920)
  • A Lost Lady — Willa Cather (1923)
  • Selected short fiction — Zora Neale Hurston
  • Selected essays — Charlotte Perkins Gilman

Legal Texts · 1915–1925

  • U.S. Supreme Court opinions on women's labor & property rights
  • State court decisions on women's suffrage challenges
  • 19th Amendment Congressional debate transcripts (1918–1919)
  • NWSA and suffrage movement legal briefs
  • Selected state legislative records, pre- and post-ratification
Analytical Frame
1915
Corpus window opens. New York votes down suffrage amendment for the second time. Wharton publishes The Custom of the Country.
1917
New York grants women the vote. Congress introduces the Susan B. Anthony Amendment for the first time since 1896.
1919
Congress passes the 19th Amendment. State ratification begins. Cather publishes My Ántonia.
1920
19th Amendment ratified. August 26, 1920. The analytical break point. Wharton publishes The Age of Innocence two months later.
1922
Adkins v. Children's Hospital — Supreme Court strikes down minimum wage law for women. The legal language of protection vs. autonomy intensifies.
1925
Corpus window closes. Five years post-Amendment. First measurements of whether the language has actually shifted.
About the Researcher

Fluent in data, law, and fiction — in that order, or any other

Most computational literary projects are built by researchers who come from one side — either the humanistic or the technical. This project requires both.

I am a data scientist with fifteen years of experience working with language at scale. A former legal aid attorney with firsthand knowledge of how law constructs meaning through text — and whose interests it serves when it chooses one word over another. And a published fiction writer (novel and edited anthology, both Foreword INDIES finalists) with a reader's attention to what language does beneath the surface of argument.

I know what legal language is doing when it calls a woman a "dependent." I know what a novelist is doing when she gives her protagonist motion or stillness. And I know how to write the code that can find those patterns across ten thousand sentences.

Fellowship Deliverables

What the fellowship year would produce

①
An open-source toolkit for comparative figurative analysis across document types — literary, legal, or otherwise — fully documented and available for other researchers to extend.
②
An interactive digital essay — public-facing, embeddable — presenting findings with live visualizations that let readers explore the corpus themselves, not just read conclusions about it.
③
A longform written piece suitable for a digital humanities or law and humanities journal — or as a work of creative nonfiction that sits inside the analysis as much as it describes it.
④
A methodological foundation for extending the analysis to other historical hinge moments: Reconstruction, the Civil Rights Act, Title IX — anywhere law and literature were writing about the same people in the same moment.

The vote changed what women were allowed to do. This project asks whether it changed how they were allowed to be described — in the rooms where law was made, and in the rooms that fiction imagined.
